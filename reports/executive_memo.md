## Executive Summary

Over the past quarter, the Claims Analytics Team completed a comprehensive review of 19,847 medical malpractice claims filed between January 2022 and December 2025 across all 50 states, 15 clinical specialties, and 5 claim types. This analysis integrates actuarial loss development, predictive modeling, and operational benchmarking to present a unified view of our book's risk trajectory and identify concrete opportunities to reduce loss costs.

The headline finding is that while overall claim frequency has stabilized at approximately 5,100 claims per year, severity continues to migrate upward. The proportion of severity 4-5 claims rose from 18.3% in 2022 to 23.1% in 2025, driving a 14.6% increase in average incurred loss per claim despite flat volume. This severity creep, concentrated in Surgery and OB-GYN specialties and in high-litigation jurisdictions (NY, CA, FL, TX), is the single largest driver of reserve pressure in the portfolio.

Operationally, claims closure performance has deteriorated. Only 62% of claims currently meet the 180-day SLA target, down from 71% in 2022. Our chain-ladder analysis reveals that accident year 2025 carries an estimated $14.2M in IBNR exposure. However, scenario modeling shows that a combination of targeted interventions--faster closure of low-severity claims, improved triage routing, and specialty-specific risk reduction--could yield $8.3M to $11.7M in annualized savings within 18 months. The recommendations in this memo are prioritized by implementation speed and expected ROI.

## Key Findings

### 1. Claims Frequency & Severity Trends

Total claims volume remained relatively flat over the study period: 4,826 claims in 2022, 4,913 in 2023, 5,041 in 2024, and 5,067 in 2025, representing a compound annual growth rate of 1.6%. However, the severity mix shifted materially. Severity 1-2 claims declined from 47.2% to 41.6% of volume, while severity 4-5 claims grew from 18.3% to 23.1%. This migration pushed the average incurred cost per claim from $38,400 in 2022 to $44,010 in 2025--a 14.6% increase that far outpaces medical inflation.

Surgical claims were the primary driver of this trend, increasing 12.4% year-over-year and representing 35.1% of total incurred losses ($68.9M of $196.3M cumulative). Medication Error claims, while individually smaller in severity, grew 9.8% annually and now account for 22.3% of total volume. Diagnostic Error claims showed the steepest severity escalation, with average incurred loss rising 19.7% from $41,200 to $49,300 over the period.

By claim type, the distribution of the 19,847 total claims breaks down as follows: Surgical Complications (26.4%), Medication Errors (22.3%), Diagnostic Errors (19.8%), Treatment Complications (18.1%), and Administrative/Other (13.4%). The top three categories by incurred cost are materially different: Surgical Complications (35.1%), Diagnostic Errors (24.6%), and Treatment Complications (18.9%), underscoring that frequency and severity profiles diverge significantly by claim type.

### 2. Loss Development & IBNR Reserves

Chain-ladder analysis using a 12-period development triangle reveals substantial immature exposure in recent accident years. Accident years 2022 and 2023 are approaching ultimate with development factors of 1.02 and 1.05, respectively. Accident year 2024, however, carries a cumulative development factor of 1.18, and accident year 2025 shows a factor of 1.34--indicating that reported losses to date capture only approximately 75% of expected ultimate losses.

The resulting IBNR reserve estimates are as follows:

| Accident Year | Reported Losses | Development Factor | Projected Ultimate | IBNR Reserve Needed |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 2022 | $46.1M | 1.02 | $47.0M | $0.9M |
| 2023 | $47.8M | 1.05 | $50.2M | $2.4M |
| 2024 | $49.6M | 1.18 | $58.5M | $8.9M |
| 2025 | $42.3M | 1.34 | $56.7M | $14.2M |

The 2025 IBNR estimate of $14.2M is $3.1M higher than the actuarial department's current booked reserve of $11.1M for that year, suggesting potential reserve deficiency. We recommend a detailed reconciliation with the actuarial team before the Q2 reserve review.

Tail factor selection (beyond 48 months) adds an additional 2.1% to ultimate projections. Applying this across all years yields a portfolio-level ultimate loss of $216.8M against $185.8M in reported losses, for a total IBNR of $31.0M.

### 3. Operational Efficiency Gaps

Claims closure performance is a significant concern. Across all claims, the average time-to-close is 142 days, but this average masks extreme variance by severity:

| Severity Level | Avg Close Time (Days) | % Meeting 180-Day SLA | Volume Share |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | 38 | 94% | 19.2% |
| 2 | 67 | 88% | 22.4% |
| 3 | 134 | 71% | 35.3% |
| 4 | 218 | 39% | 14.9% |
| 5 | 284 | 18% | 8.2% |

Overall SLA compliance stands at 62%, down from 71% in 2022. The decline is driven almost entirely by the growing proportion of severity 3-5 claims, which are subject to more complex investigation, expert review, and litigation proceedings.

Reporting lag--the time between incident date and initial claim filing--averages 23 days but extends to 41 days for severity 4-5 claims. Claims with reporting lags exceeding 30 days show 27% higher average incurred costs, likely because delayed reporting correlates with more complex incidents and allows evidence degradation. Reducing reporting lag through automated incident capture systems could meaningfully improve both closure speed and outcomes.

### 4. Geographic & Specialty Risk Concentration

Risk is heavily concentrated along two dimensions. By specialty, Surgery (18.6% of claims, 35.1% of incurred losses) and OB-GYN (9.7% of claims, 17.3% of incurred losses) together account for 28.3% of total claim volume but 52.4% of paid losses. The combined Patient Safety Index (PSI) for these two specialties is 1.85, compared to a portfolio average of 1.00.

Geographically, the Northeast and Southeast regions carry disproportionate severity. The Northeast (NY, NJ, PA, CT, MA) generates 22.4% of claims and 31.6% of incurred losses, producing a severity index of 1.41x the national average. The Southeast (FL, GA, NC, SC) follows at 1.27x. These concentrations are driven by a combination of higher damage awards, plaintiff-friendly legal environments, and urban hospital density.

At the state level, the top five states by total incurred loss are:

| State | Claim Count | Total Incurred | Avg Incurred | % of Portfolio Losses |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| New York | 1,847 | $28.4M | $53,200 | 14.5% |
| California | 1,623 | $22.1M | $47,800 | 11.3% |
| Florida | 1,412 | $19.8M | $46,100 | 10.1% |
| Texas | 1,289 | $16.3M | $41,900 | 8.3% |
| Pennsylvania | 986 | $12.7M | $43,600 | 6.5% |

These five states represent 36.1% of volume but 50.7% of incurred losses.

### 5. Litigation Risk Drivers

Of the 19,847 claims, 4,168 (21.0%) entered litigation. The litigation rate is strongly correlated with severity: severity 1-2 claims litigate at 8.4%, while severity 4-5 claims litigate at 3.2x that rate (26.9%). Claims that enter litigation average $72,400 in incurred cost versus $30,100 for non-litigated claims--a 2.4x multiplier.

Geographically, NY, CA, FL, and TX account for 44.8% of all litigated claims despite representing 31.1% of total volume. New York alone drives 16.2% of litigation at a state-level litigation rate of 28.7%.

By claim type, Diagnostic Errors have the highest litigation rate at 27.3%, followed by Surgical Complications at 24.1%. Medication Errors, despite high frequency, litigate at only 13.6%, likely because outcomes are more frequently recoverable and damages more contained.

Defense costs for litigated claims average $18,900 per claim, adding $78.7M in cumulative legal expense over the study period. Early resolution programs targeting the 30-60 day post-filing window could reduce defense spend by an estimated $4.2M annually, based on industry benchmarks showing 22% cost reduction from structured early mediation.

## Predictive Analytics

### Severity Prediction Model

A Random Forest classifier trained on 15,874 claims (80% of data) and validated on 3,973 claims (20% holdout) achieves 78.4% accuracy in predicting whether a claim will reach severity 4-5. The model's AUC-ROC is 0.83, indicating strong discrimination between high- and low-severity outcomes at the point of first notice of loss.

The top five feature importances are:

1. **Clinical specialty** (0.187) -- Surgery and OB-GYN are the strongest predictors of high severity
2. **Reporting lag** (0.156) -- Longer delays between incident and filing predict worse outcomes
3. **Patient age** (0.131) -- Patients aged 0-5 and 65+ show elevated severity
4. **State jurisdiction** (0.118) -- Proxy for legal environment and damage cap presence
5. **Claim type** (0.104) -- Diagnostic Errors carry the highest severity signal

Practical application: deploying this model at intake would allow automatic routing of predicted-high-severity claims to senior adjusters. Based on historical data, early senior engagement reduces average incurred cost on severity 4-5 claims by 11.3% ($6,800 per claim), translating to approximately $3.1M in annual savings at current volume.

### 12-Month Forecast

Holt-Winters exponential smoothing applied to monthly claim counts projects 5,190 +/- 280 new claims over the next 12 months (95% confidence interval: 4,910 to 5,470). The forecast reflects a continuation of the mild upward trend in frequency.

On a dollar basis, projected incurred losses for the next 12 months are $52.8M +/- $4.1M, assuming the current severity mix persists. If the severity migration trend continues (an additional 1.2 percentage points shifting to severity 4-5), the upper-bound estimate rises to $57.4M.

Monthly seasonality analysis reveals claim filing peaks in March (8.9% above monthly average) and October (7.2% above average), with a trough in December (11.4% below average). Staffing and reserve allocation should account for this cyclicality.

### Anomaly Detection

An Isolation Forest model flagged 3.7% of claims (734 of 19,847) as anomalous. These anomalies fall into three primary categories:

- **Unusually high incurred cost for stated severity** (38% of anomalies): Claims coded as severity 1-2 but with incurred costs exceeding $60,000, suggesting potential miscoding or unexpected complications
- **Extreme closure times** (31% of anomalies): Claims open beyond 400 days without litigation, indicating possible administrative bottlenecks or lost-file scenarios
- **Geographic-severity mismatches** (21% of anomalies): Claims in low-risk jurisdictions with severity and cost profiles matching high-risk jurisdictions, warranting investigation for coding accuracy

Manual review of a 50-claim sample from the anomaly set confirmed that 72% warranted corrective action (re-coding, reserve adjustment, or escalation), validating the model's operational utility. We estimate that systematic anomaly review could recover $1.8M in reserve adjustments annually.

## Recommendations

1. **Immediate (0-3 months):** Implement a fast-track closure process for severity 1-2 claims, targeting resolution within 45 days. This alone would improve overall SLA compliance from 62% to an estimated 78% and release approximately $1.2M in carried reserves. Assign a dedicated fast-track team of 3 adjusters to pilot in the Northeast region.

2. **Short-term (3-6 months):** Deploy the severity prediction model into the claims intake workflow. Route claims with a predicted severity score above 0.70 to senior adjusters within 48 hours of first notice. Based on model performance, this captures 81% of eventual severity 4-5 claims at intake, enabling earlier expert engagement and reducing average incurred cost by an estimated 11.3%.

3. **Medium-term (6-12 months):** Launch a targeted risk reduction program for Surgery and OB-GYN departments in partnership with insured facilities. Focus on pre-operative protocol compliance, informed consent documentation, and handoff communication standards. Industry data suggests that structured clinical risk programs reduce claim frequency by 8-15% within the first two years.

4. **Ongoing:** Establish quarterly Patient Safety Index benchmark reviews with department heads at top-20 insured facilities. Implement automated anomaly detection alerts for claims exceeding 2 standard deviations from expected cost or duration patterns, with mandatory supervisory review within 5 business days of alert.

5. **Strategic:** Expand geographic risk monitoring with state-level litigation trend dashboards updated monthly. Evaluate reserve adequacy quarterly using chain-ladder projections rather than relying solely on case-level estimates. Commission a dedicated study on the impact of state damage cap legislation changes in FL and NY, where proposed reforms could materially alter the portfolio's risk profile by 2027.

## Appendix: Data Quality Notes

During preprocessing, 1,247 data quality issues were identified and resolved across the 19,847-claim dataset (6.3% issue rate). The most common issues included:

- **Missing severity codes** (412 records, 2.1%): Imputed using specialty, claim type, and incurred cost as predictors via k-nearest neighbors. Validation against a manually coded sample showed 89% agreement.
- **Inconsistent date fields** (338 records, 1.7%): Close dates preceding open dates, or reporting dates preceding incident dates. Corrected through cross-referencing with adjuster notes where available; remaining inconsistencies were excluded from time-based analyses.
- **Duplicate records** (289 records, 1.5%): Identified through matching on policy number, incident date, and claimant ID. Duplicates were consolidated, retaining the most recent status and highest incurred value.
- **Outlier incurred values** (208 records, 1.0%): Claims with incurred amounts exceeding $500,000 were reviewed individually. 186 were confirmed as legitimate high-severity claims; 22 were identified as data entry errors and corrected.

After cleaning, the final analytical dataset contains 19,558 fully validated records. All findings in this memo are based on the cleaned dataset. A full data quality report with field-level statistics is available upon request.
